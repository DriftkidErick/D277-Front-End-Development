Overview and History

Rhode Island, the smallest U.S. state, is part of New England and bordered by Massachusetts and Connecticut. Rhode Island Sound supports its fishing industry. First settled by Europeans in 1636, it received a charter from King Charles II in 1663. Despite suffering in King Philip's War, it played a key role in opposing British customs laws, aiding the American Revolution. In 1790, it became the 13th state to ratify the Constitution after the Bill of Rights was included. The original charter lasted until Dorrâ€™s Rebellion in 1842, which extended voting rights. Samuel Slater's 1790 mill in Pawtucket started the U.S. Industrial Revolution. Manufacturing, including jewelry, textiles, and electronics, remains important. City Information

Providence, the capital and most populous city of Rhode Island, is one of the oldest cities in New England, founded in 1636 by Roger Williams, incorporated on November 5, 1832. Providence is located at the head of Narragansett Bay, with the Providence River running into the bay through the center of the city, it developed as a busy port and was one of the first cities in the U.S. to industrialize, becoming known for its textile, machine tool, jewelry, and silverware industries. Today, Providence's economy has shifted towards service industries, supported by eight hospitals and eight institutions of higher learning, though it retains some manufacturing. As of the 2020 census, Providence has a population of 190,934, making it the third-most-populous city in New England, and its metropolitan area, with over 1.6 million residents, is the second largest in New England.

(Source: Wikipedia)
